NPS withdrawal rules determine exactly how much of your retirement corpus you can access as cash, how much gets locked into an annuity, and what the taxman takes at each stage. If you have been contributing to NPS for years — or are deciding whether to start — knowing these exit rules before you need them is the difference between a smooth retirement and a surprise lock-in. This guide breaks down every NPS exit stage with real corpus numbers, so you can see precisely how much reaches your bank account.

## What is NPS and when can you withdraw

The National Pension System (NPS) is a voluntary, defined-contribution retirement scheme regulated by PFRDA. You contribute during your working years, the money is invested in a mix of equity, corporate bonds and government securities, and the corpus grows with compounding until you reach the exit point.

NPS is designed as a retirement product, which means the money is locked in for the long haul. You cannot withdraw freely like a savings account or even an FD. The PFRDA regulations define three exit windows:

1. **Normal exit at maturity (age 60)** — the standard retirement exit
2. **Premature exit before age 60** — allowed after completing 5 years in NPS
3. **Partial withdrawal** — for specific life events, without fully exiting the scheme

Each window has its own withdrawal percentages, annuity requirements and tax treatment. If you are planning your retirement corpus, understanding these NPS withdrawal rules alongside other savings targets — like [how much to save monthly for ₹1 crore](/blog/how-much-should-you-save-monthly-for-1-crore-in-10-years) — gives you a realistic picture of your post-retirement cash flow.

## NPS withdrawal at maturity age 60: the 60-40 split

When you turn 60, you can exit NPS and access your corpus. This is the normal maturity exit, and the rules are straightforward:

- **60% of corpus** — withdraw as lump sum, tax-free
- **40% of corpus** — must be used to purchase an annuity from an IRDAI-registered annuity provider
- **If corpus is up to ₹5 lakh** — you can withdraw 100% as lump sum, no annuity required

### Real corpus scenario at age 60

Let's say you have built a corpus of ₹50 lakh by age 60. Here is how the 60-40 split works:

| Component | Percentage | Amount | Tax treatment |
|-----------|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Lump sum withdrawal | 60% | ₹30,00,000 | Tax-free |
| Annuity purchase | 40% | ₹20,00,000 | Locked in annuity |
| Total corpus | 100% | ₹50,00,000 | — |

You get ₹30 lakh in your bank account as a tax-free lump sum. The remaining ₹20 lakh goes to an annuity provider, and you receive a monthly pension from it — which is taxable.

If your corpus is larger, say ₹1 crore:

| Component | Percentage | Amount | Tax treatment |
|-----------|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Lump sum withdrawal | 60% | ₹60,00,000 | Tax-free |
| Annuity purchase | 40% | ₹40,00,000 | Locked in annuity |
| Total corpus | 100% | ₹1,00,00,000 | — |

You can also choose to withdraw the 60% in a phased manner over several years rather than all at once. This is called a "systematic lump sum withdrawal" and can help you manage cash flow better — though the entire 60% remains tax-free regardless of whether you take it in one go or in instalments.

### What if corpus is under ₹5 lakh at age 60

If your NPS corpus is ₹5 lakh or less at maturity, PFRDA allows you to withdraw 100% as a lump sum. No annuity purchase is required. This is useful for people who joined NPS late or contributed small amounts.

## Premature exit from NPS before age 60

NPS is built for retirement, so exiting before 60 comes with stricter conditions. You can make a premature exit only after completing 5 years in the scheme. The NPS exit rules for premature withdrawal are:

- **20% of corpus** — withdraw as lump sum, tax-free
- **80% of corpus** — must be used to purchase an annuity
- **If corpus is up to ₹5 lakh** — withdraw 100% as lump sum, no annuity required
